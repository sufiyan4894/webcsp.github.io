ABSTRACT


The concepts of joint and nuclear families represent two distinct social structures with unique characteristics, advantages, and challenges. A joint family, also known as an extended family, typically consists of multiple generations living under one roof, including grandparents, parents, children, and sometimes other relatives. This family structure promotes strong familial bonds, shared responsibilities, and collective decision-making, providing a robust support system for its members. On the other hand, a nuclear family comprises two parents and their children living independently. This setup fosters greater autonomy, privacy, and individual freedom, often allowing for more efficient resource management and stronger parent-child relationships. However, it may lack the extensive support network inherent in joint families. This abstract explores the dynamics of both family structures, comparing their sociocultural implications, economic aspects, and impact on individual development, ultimately highlighting the evolving nature of family systems in contemporary society.
